Learning to Order

GNN-Based Deep Reinforcement Learning Policies
for Autoregressive Graph Models

Alysson Amaral da Silva · André Luís Vignatti

Block 1 · Why this paper

Graph generation in one slide

Learn a distribution \(p(G)\) over graphs and sample new ones that preserve the properties of the training data.

Molecules Social networks Knowledge graphs Circuits Biological interactions
Autoregressive generators (DGMG, GraphRNN, GRAN, GraphGPT, G2PT…) build a graph step by step, one node/edge decision at a time.

We focus on DGMG as a controlled environment, but the framing is generator-agnostic.

Block 1 · Why this paper

The catch: graphs are unordered, sequences are not

  • A graph with \(n\) nodes has up to \(n!\) equivalent label permutations.
  • An autoregressive model must factorize a chosen sequence of actions.
  • So we pick a node ordering \(\sigma\) and translate \((G,\sigma)\) into actions AddNode, AddEdge, ChooseDest, StopEdge, StopNode.
  • Different \(\sigma\) ↠ different action sequences ↠ different supervised problems for the very same graph.
\[ p_\phi(G) = \sum_{\sigma \in \mathcal{O}_G} p_\phi(G \mid \sigma)\, p(\sigma) \]

Marginalizing over all orderings is intractable (factorial). In practice generators fix a heuristic \(\sigma\) and treat it as canonical.

Central question
"Is node ordering a neutral preprocessing detail,
or a modeling choice we should be optimizing?"

Claim: ordering is a first-class modeling variable, learn it.

Block 2 · Background

Deterministic orderings we benchmark against

Degree BFS DFS Degeneracy LexBFS MCS Spectral Random

Same DGMG architecture, same data, same protocol — only the graph→sequence ordering changes. This isolates the effect of \(\sigma\) on the generator.

Empirical lesson: no universal winner

Topology Best ordering Intuition
Cycles DFS / LexBFS Path-like traversal + clean ring closure
Trees BFS Parent–child locality on the expansion frontier
Stars Degree Inserting the hub first trivializes every later decision
Barabási–Albert None dominates Trade-offs across size, connectivity, hub structure
On BA, no fixed heuristic wins valid-size and connectivity and hub structure at the same time.

This is the gap the paper attacks.

Block 3 · Main contribution

Idea

"Replace the hand-crafted ordering with a graph-conditioned policy trained by reinforcement learning, where the reward is the quality of the graphs produced by the downstream generator."

Concretely: an attention-based GNN scores nodes; we pick them one at a time; DGMG trains on the induced sequences; REINFORCE updates the policy.

Block 3 · Main contribution

Node ordering as a Markov Decision Process

  • State \(s_t = (G, S_t)\): fixed graph + nodes already chosen.
  • Action: pick \(u_t \in V \setminus S_t\).
  • Transition: deterministic, \(S_{t+1} = S_t \cup \{u_t\}\).
  • Episode: ends when \(|S_t| = n\), yielding a full permutation \(\sigma\).
  • Reward: delayed, computed only after DGMG is trained on \(\sigma\).
\[ \pi_\theta(\sigma \mid G) = \prod_{t=1}^{n} \pi_\theta(u_t \mid G, S_t) \]

Episodic, \(\gamma = 1\). Permutation built one node at a time, conditioned on the whole graph.

Block 3 · Main contribution

Bilevel objective

Lower level — generator

Train DGMG parameters \(\phi\) on sequences induced by orderings sampled from the policy.

\[ \phi^\star(\theta) = \arg\min_\phi \mathbb{E}_{G \sim \mathcal{D}}\, \mathbb{E}_{\sigma \sim \pi_\theta} [\mathcal{L}_{\text{train}}(\phi, \mathcal{T}(G,\sigma))] \]
Upper level — policy

Maximize downstream reward at the current generator equilibrium.

\[ \theta^\star = \arg\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{G \sim \mathcal{D}}\, \mathbb{E}_{\sigma \sim \pi_\theta} [R(G, \sigma, \phi^\star(\theta))] \]

Solving the inner problem exactly is too expensive — we alternate stochastic updates instead.

Block 3 · Main contribution

Composite reward: NLL is not enough

\[ R(\tau) = R_{\text{pred}}(\tau) + \lambda\, R_{\text{struct}}(\tau) \]
  • \(R_{\text{pred}}\): negative validation NLL of DGMG — "is this ordering easy to model?"
  • \(R_{\text{struct}}\): family-specific validity on samples — "do the generated graphs look right?"
  • For BA: \(R_{\text{struct}}(\tau) = \text{valid\_size\_ratio} \cdot \text{connected\_ratio}\).
  • \(\lambda\): trade-off between likelihood fit and structural fidelity.

Without the structural term the policy could find orderings with low NLL that still produce structurally broken graphs (small, disconnected, no hubs).

Block 3 · Main contribution

Policy architecture

Graph encoder (GNN) → Selection mask \(m_v^{(t)}\) → Global readout \(h_G\) → MLP score \(z_v^{(t)}\) → Masked softmax
\[ \pi_\theta(u_t = v \mid s_t) = \frac{\exp(z_v^{(t)})}{\sum_{u \in V \setminus S_t} \exp(z_u^{(t)})} \]
  • Encoder consumes \((G, S_t)\) and per-node features (e.g. normalized degree + selected flag).
  • Already-selected nodes are masked out, so the policy only assigns mass to valid actions.
  • Sampled at training time (exploration), greedy at evaluation time.
Block 3 · Main contribution

Two GNN variants for the policy

GCN RL ordering
  • Encoder: GCN, fixed neighbor aggregation.
  • 2 message-passing rounds, small hidden dim.
  • Random initial policy trajectory.
  • Proves the framework is feasible.
Attention RL ordering (this paper)
  • Encoder: transformer-style attention over nodes.
  • 3 graph layers — larger receptive field.
  • Warm start from the Degree ordering at iteration 1.
  • RL refines the hub-aware prior, not relearns it.

Same MDP, same reward, same alternating loop — only the policy network and its warm start change.

Block 3 · Main contribution

Policy gradient (REINFORCE) with baseline

\[ \nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{G \sim \mathcal{D}}\, \mathbb{E}_{\sigma \sim \pi_\theta} \!\left[ \left(\sum_{t=1}^{n}\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(u_t \mid s_t)\right) \bigl(R(G,\sigma,\phi) - b(G)\bigr) \right] \]
  • Discrete actions + non-differentiable reward (we have to train DGMG to evaluate \(\sigma\)).
  • Score-function estimator — no backprop through the generator.
  • Baseline \(b(G)\) reduces variance; optional entropy bonus \(\beta\,H[\pi_\theta]\) keeps exploration alive.
Block 3 · Main contribution

Alternating optimization loop

Sample \(\sigma \sim \pi_\theta\) → Translate \(\mathcal{T}(G,\sigma)\) → Train DGMG \(\phi\) → Evaluate \(R(\tau)\) → REINFORCE update \(\theta\)
↺ repeat for \(I\) outer iterations, \(K\) policy steps each
  • Iteration 1 (Attention RL): serialize with Degree — informative starting point.
  • Iterations \(>1\): the current policy proposes \(\sigma\) for every training graph.
  • Policy and generator co-adapt: better \(\sigma\) shapes a better \(\phi\), and vice versa.
Block 4 · Experiments

Experimental setup (Barabási–Albert)

Why BA?

  • Sparse, scale-free, heavy-tailed degree distribution.
  • A "good" ordering must balance size, connectivity, and hub structure.
  • Common stress test in the graph-generation literature.

Dataset

  • 6000 train / 750 val / 750 test BA graphs
  • \(m_{BA} = 2\), \(N \in [20, 60]\)

Metrics

  • valid-size ratio — fraction with size in the training interval
  • connected ratio — fraction of fully connected samples
  • degree Gini — inequality of the degree distribution
  • hubiness — concentration of mass on top-degree nodes
  • validation NLL — monitored for convergence/stopping

Block 4 · Experiments

Two head-to-heads worth staring at

Attention RL vs. BFS
  • Both reach ~1.0 connectivity.
  • BFS collapses average size to 27.3 (below training range).
  • Attention RL keeps size at 41.4 — well inside the BA distribution.
Attention RL vs. Degree (its warm start)
  • Same perfect valid-size ratio.
  • Connectivity: 0.6490 → 0.9960.
  • Hubiness: 0.3326 → 0.3855.
  • RL does not just memorize Degree; it refines it.
The learned policy combines strengths of multiple heuristics — per graph, conditioned on topology.
Block 4 · Experiments

Training dynamics

  • Loss is oscillatory — expected from an alternating RL–DGMG loop.
  • Best validation checkpoint at outer iteration 61: the policy keeps exploring instead of collapsing early.
  • The stronger encoder + warm start let optimization survive a much longer horizon than the GCN variant.
  • Convergence criterion: difference between consecutive validation losses across outer iterations.
Block 5 · Takeaways

What I want you to remember

  • Ordering is a modeling choice, not preprocessing. For autoregressive graph generators, \(\sigma\) decides the supervised problem.
  • No fixed heuristic dominates on BA. You pay for size, connectivity, or hubs — pick two.
  • Attention RL ordering closes the gap. Same MDP framing as GCN RL, but a stronger encoder + warm start lifts connectivity from 0.63 to 1.0 with no loss elsewhere.
  • Generator-agnostic recipe. Demonstrated on DGMG, but applies to any autoregressive graph model that needs a node order.
Block 5 · Takeaways

Limitations and what's next

Limitations

  • Evaluated on synthetic BA graphs only.
  • Outer loop is expensive — each RL update trains DGMG.
  • REINFORCE has high variance even with baseline.
  • Reward design is BA-specific; needs to be re-engineered per family.

Future work

  • Real datasets: molecules, biological networks, knowledge graphs.
  • Variance reduction: actor-critic, importance sampling, GRPO-style estimators.
  • Interpretability of the learned ordering — what does the policy actually do?
  • Plug into other generators (GraphRNN, GraphGPT, G2PT…).
